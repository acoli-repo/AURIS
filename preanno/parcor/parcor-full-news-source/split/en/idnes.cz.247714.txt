You are not as negative as the French, says a Muslim living in Prague.
You are from France.
Where have you lived before you moved to Prague?
My parents are originally from Algeria.
I was born in the south of France, near the Spanish border.
So I moved around quite a bit.
I lived in Toulouse, Lyon and Marseille.
In France, I studied journalism, thanks to which I managed to get a job with a magazine.
Why did you then decide to move in another country?
I started to feel suffocated in France.
I just did not want to live there anymore.
Mainly because of the atmosphere between people.
The French people came across as overly negative.
People in France are generally quite negative, but it has become much worse lately.
It is interesting that you mention negativity.
There are many people you say this about the Czechs.
Believe me, you are not as negative as the French.
Some people who come to France as tourists, for example, may not be so aware of it.
But I had enough.
Everybody keeps talking about money, for example.
People feel that they do not have enough money, despite the fact that the minimum wage in France is EUR 1,500, which in my view is quite a lot.
Now there is another problem with behaviour towards Muslims,
which is beyond me.
For example, in the street where I lived, my non-Muslim neighbour started to look down on me just because I am a Muslim.
As if I was some kind of evil.
As a result, I no longer felt welcome in France,
even though the rest of my family stayed there.
So you decided to move to another country.
Why did you choose the Czech Republic?
I was looking for a job in a different country, and I found an opening at Air France here in Prague.
I admit that I did not know anything about Prague at the beginning.
I knew something about the Czech Republic as my dad told me about what happened in Czechoslovakia, but I did not know what to expect from Prague.
I have now been living here for three years, and I cannot imagine living anywhere else.
Was it a big difference when you moved from France to Prague?
Mainly because of the different atmosphere.
Maybe it is because I do not speak Czech, so I cannot catch some of the negative reactions or insults, but I feel that people have more freedom here.
I feel I can do anything, dress in any way I like, and nobody will judge me.
In France, people keep starring at you in an unpleasant way because you dress differently, etc.
In Prague, I began to feel really free.
In my opinion, locals do not seem to care much about these things.
Where do you work now?
After I left Air France, I worked for a while for a travel website.
After that I founded a website together with a friend, which can be used by people to find and offer shared accommodation.
We currently only operate in Prague but we would like to expand to cover the whole of Central Europe.
Do you have any Czech friends in Prague?
I have two very close friends, who are half Czech, and two female friends who are Czech.
I must say that Czechs tend to keep their distance at first.
They do not let just anyone get too close,
which is quite different where I grew up.
In the south, everybody is hugging and kissing each other on the cheek.
It is different here, but I understand that this is a cultural thing, so I try to adjust.
You mentioned that you do not speak Czech.
Does the language barrier present a problem for you?
Quite the contrary, I find it is really easy here in Prague because virtually everyone speaks English here.
Even in convenience stores and supermarkets.
I would like to learn Czech, but I do not have time
and because it is relatively easy to communicate in English, I do not have the incentive to learn the language quickly.
Every time when I have a problem, somebody comes along who can help me.
Did you come across some negative reactions?
Maybe because you are a Muslim?
By and large, I did not.
Maybe it is also because I work in an international environment which is more tolerant.
I can think of one experience though.
My supervisor, wo was Czech, once told me about the French football team and said something along the lines of:
While I found that comment a bit offensive,
I never had anybody having a problem with me because of me being a Muslim.
A colleague once warned me, however, about anti-Muslim demonstrations.
He told me to avoid the city centre because they would only be hurling insults at me.
Anti-Muslim demonstrations are partly related to terrorist attacks.
What do you think about the current situation?
Are you afraid that something similar to Paris, Nice or Brussels could happen here in Prague?
I feel safe in Prague.
The problem is that nowadays anything can happen anywhere.
Until recently, I could not imagine that something like that could happen in France.
And this does not only concern Muslims.
What is more, Islam does not condone violence at all.
In my view, people who kill other people are not Muslims.
According to the Quran, no human can kill another human. Only God can kill.
What do you think about terrorists using your faith to justify their actions?
It is tough.
I feel shame and guilt, even though I should not feel like that at all.
I try not to follow the news about violence too much but every time I read something, I feel the need to apologise for something that is really not my fault.
I am a completely different person, so why should I apologize for the terrorists?
We met on náměstí Jiřího z Poděbrad.
On Wednesday, Czech Muslims, along with Christians demonstrated here against violence.
Did you know about it?
No, I was not aware of it.
But I understand why people feel the need to show that they are against violence.
I do not think that they have to do something like this.
Why should they feel guilty for something they did not cause?
Do you come across other Muslims in Prague?
My colleagues are, for example, from Egypt and other Muslim countries.
They tend to be a bit more religious than I am, so we sometimes have fierce discussions about Islam, because we see Islam quite differently.
I come from France, so I went through a different education system, whereas they have been taught Islam in school.
We have different attitudes, but we have respect for one another.
People sometimes associate Islam with the subordinate position of women.
What do you think about it?
I definitely do not feel subordinate.
Quite the contrary.
I am a woman and I am strong.
Does it have anything to do with the fact that you do not cover your head
and wear a hijab for example?
It has something to do with how I was raised.
My mother, for example, is a devoted Muslim.
She prays five times a day, but she does not wear a headscarf.
I was always taught that faith is a purely personal matter.
It depends on how one interprets it.
When you read the Quran, you should make up your own mind.
When I read a passage in the Quran which says that God told the Prophet to hide his wives from the eyes of the others, I said to myself,
On the other hand, I would wear headscarf if I travelled to Iran or Saudi Arabia, where headscarves are mandatory.
Do you plan to stay in Prague?
So far, yes.
I have never felt so good anywhere else.
I thought for a while about Poland, but I feel great in Prague.
I could even imagine raising my children here.
In my view, Prague is an ideal city.
