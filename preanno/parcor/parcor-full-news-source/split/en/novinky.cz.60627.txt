Pay to go to jail.
In Scotland, you can go to prison without committing a crime.
For many centuries, Inveraray, the seat of the Duke of Argyll, was the principal county town of Argyll.
From the mid-18th century the courts met in the Town House on Front Street, and the ground floor below the courtroom served as the county prison.
The conditions were terrible for prisoners and unsurprisingly, there were many escape attempts and
some of which were successful.
The town representatives, therefore, decided to move the prison to a new location.
The new building was completed in 1848. It was much more prisoner-friendly as it was heated and lit by gas.
It did not stay open for long and it finally closed by the end of August 1889 for economic reasons, as by this time, the smaller county jails were expensive and inefficient to run.
Prisoner stories and ghost hunting
The prison opened its doors to the public hundred years later. Visitors can see for themselves how different the conditions were in the old building compared to the new building.
Guides in period costumes make the experience even more authentic.
Visitors can see the torture chamber where they can learn about the many forms of punishment inflicted on criminals, whether it involved breaking their fingers or branding theme with hot irons.
During the tour, tourists have the opportunity to have the warder lock them up in a cell, mainly as a photo op but also to experience for themselves what it is like to be behind bars.
Moreover, the Inveraray Jail has a reputation as one of Scotland’s most haunted locations.
It has already been the destination of numerous groups dedicated to researching paranormal activities, and the museum continues to cater for people interested in ghost hunts.
Such a night will set you back at least 300 pounds, which is roughly 9,500 Czech crowns.
Inveraray is not only a popular tourist attraction, it is also renowned for great food, surrounding scenery and history.
